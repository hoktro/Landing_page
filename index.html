Flyweight Pattern

    A REAL-WORLD PROBLEM

    Before we commence, let’s consider a problem. Imagine you are working in a programming team to come up with a simple video game that has plenty of player and they are shooting each other. Because of the customer’s requirement, your game may contains a bunch of elements such as players, bullets, magics, e.t.c and other attractive effects at the same time. Note that the events should be handled independently and smoothly. Your team has worked pretty hard to find out the following naive solution.

    NAIVE SOLUTION

    Obviously, you need define a class particle including all necessary attribution such as coordinate, name, speed, color and sprite of an object in the game. Secondly, the Game class responsible for driving game. In addition, there is a class makes game action such as fire or defend, which is unit class.

    PROBLEM

    Basically, the game is finished. Every needed functions are implemented and they work perfectly on your machine. However, after you sent your program to the tester, you received a rejected response. According to your tester, the game cannot be played so long on his machine. Particularly, after a certain time, the game is terminated and thrown bunch of errors. The discussion on what was happening took a significant time to find out the problem. It turns out that the tester’s computer hardware (i.e Main Memory) does not sufficient the memory cost of the game. It is clearly that the memory cost is extremely expensive if you have a enormous amount of elements.

    FLYWEIGHT PATTERN

    According to the book ”Design Patterns: Elements of Reusable Object-Oriented Software” by Erich Gamma, Richard Helm, Ralph Johnson, and John Vlissides, the intent of Flyweight Pattern is: ”Use sharing to support large numbers of fine-grained objects efficiently”.
